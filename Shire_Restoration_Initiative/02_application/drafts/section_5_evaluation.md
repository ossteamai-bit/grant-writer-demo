# SECTION 5: EVALUATION & IMPACT
## The Shire Restoration Initiative

**Scoring Target: 10 points**
- Outcomes are SMART (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound) (4 pts)
- Evaluation methods are appropriate and rigorous (3 pts)
- Plan includes community feedback and course correction (3 pts)

---

## EVALUATION PHILOSOPHY

The Shire Restoration Initiative will employ a comprehensive evaluation approach measuring both quantitative outcomes (homes rebuilt, trees planted, economic indicators) and qualitative impacts (community healing, cultural revitalization, hope restored). Evaluation serves three purposes:

1. **Accountability:** Demonstrate to Elrond Foundation and community that resources produced promised results
2. **Continuous Improvement:** Identify what's working and what needs adjustment during implementation
3. **Knowledge Building:** Document lessons learned for other post-war communities in Middle-Earth

**Community-Centered Evaluation:** Hobbits will participate in evaluation design, data collection, and interpretation—ensuring evaluation respects community priorities and cultural values.

By Month 15, Hobbiton Meeting Hall will be fully restored and hosting monthly community gatherings with average attendance of 200+ Hobbits.

**Outcome 1.5 - Workforce Development:**
By Month 18, 40 previously unemployed Hobbits will have completed construction training and 80% will be employed in building trades or related work.

## EVALUATION METHODS & DATA COLLECTION

### Quantitative Measures

**1. Physical Infrastructure Tracking (Monthly)**
- **Method:** Direct count and photographic documentation
- **Metrics:**
  - Number of homes rebuilt (target: 13)
  - Number of homes repaired (target: 87+)
  - Number of trees planted (target: 2,000)
  - Number of trees surviving (target: 90%+)
  - Acres of farmland remediated and certified (target: 450)
- **Responsible:** Data Coordinator maintains comprehensive tracking spreadsheet updated monthly
- **Reporting:** Included in quarterly reports to EFMEP with photographs

**2. Service Delivery Statistics (Monthly)**
- **Method:** Program attendance and participation records
- **Metrics:**
  - Number of individuals receiving counseling
  - Number of counseling sessions delivered
  - Number participating in support groups
  - Number attending community events
  - Number completing apprenticeships/training programs
- **Responsible:** Each program coordinator tracks participants; Data Coordinator compiles
- **Reporting:** Monthly summaries; quarterly aggregate reports

**3. Economic Indicators (Quarterly)**
- **Method:** Surveys of households and businesses
- **Metrics:**
  - Unemployment rate (survey of working-age Hobbits)
  - Farm revenue (farmer self-reporting)
  - Number of operating businesses (direct count)
  - Food insecurity rate (standardized food security survey)
  - Trade volume (market vendor surveys)
- **Responsible:** Data Coordinator conducts quarterly economic survey of 200-household sample
- **Reporting:** Quarterly reports showing trends over time

**4. Population and Migration (Quarterly)**
- **Method:** Tracking through Shire government records
- **Metrics:**
  - Number of families leaving Shire
  - Number of families returning to Shire
  - Net migration (positive or negative)
- **Responsible:** Shire government provides data; Data Coordinator analyzes
- **Reporting:** Quarterly migration reports

### Qualitative Measures

**1. Mental Health Assessments (Pre/Mid/Post)**
- **Method:** Validated anxiety and trauma scales administered by counselors
- **Timing:**
  - Pre: Before counseling begins
  - Mid: Month 9 (halfway point)
  - Post: Month 18 (completion)
- **Metrics:**
  - Anxiety symptom scores
  - PTSD symptom severity
  - Self-reported wellbeing
  - Functional impairment levels
- **Responsible:** Gondorian counselors administer; Rosie compiles results
- **Confidentiality:** Individual data kept confidential; only aggregate results reported
- **Analysis:** Pre-post comparison showing change over time

**2. Community Perception Surveys (Quarterly)**
- **Method:** Written/oral surveys of 200-household random sample
- **Timing:** Baseline (Month 1), then Months 6, 12, 18
- **Questions:**
  - "The Shire looks like home again" (% agree)
  - "I feel hopeful about the Shire's future" (% agree)
  - "I fear future attacks" (% agree)
  - "I plan to stay in the Shire long-term" (% agree)
  - "Community members help each other" (% agree)
  - "I am proud of Hobbit culture" (% agree)
- **Responsible:** Data Coordinator conducts surveys; volunteers assist with oral surveys for Hobbits with limited literacy
- **Analysis:** Track trends over time; demographic breakdowns (age, gender, location)

**3. Participant Interviews (Ongoing)**
- **Method:** In-depth interviews with project participants
- **Sample:** 30 participants representing diverse experiences (displaced families, apprentices, counseling recipients, farmers, etc.)
- **Timing:** Mid-project (Month 9) and end (Month 18)
- **Questions:** Open-ended questions about experiences, benefits, challenges, suggestions
- **Responsible:** Merry (Community Engagement) conducts interviews
- **Analysis:** Qualitative coding for themes; stories for reporting

**4. Focus Groups (Semi-Annual)**
- **Method:** Small group discussions with specific populations
- **Groups:** Elders, youth, farmers, business owners, trauma survivors, women
- **Timing:** Months 6, 12, 18
- **Purpose:** Gather detailed feedback on programs; identify emerging needs; generate improvement ideas
- **Responsible:** Merry facilitates; Rosie co-facilitates for trauma survivor groups
- **Documentation:** Detailed notes; themes identified; recommendations generated

**5. Partner Feedback (Quarterly)**
- **Method:** Structured feedback from all partners
- **Questions:** Partnership functioning, areas for improvement, concerns, successes
- **Timing:** After each quarterly Partnership Council meeting
- **Responsible:** Gandalf collects feedback
- **Use:** Improve partnership coordination; address concerns proactively

**6. Photo Documentation (Ongoing)**
- **Method:** Systematic photography of project sites
- **Purpose:** Visual documentation of progress (before/after); storytelling for reports
- **Timing:** Monthly photos of consistent sites; special event documentation
- **Responsible:** Data Coordinator maintains photo archive

## COMMUNITY FEEDBACK & COURSE CORRECTION

Evaluation isn't just about measuring outcomes—it's about using information to improve the project continuously. The Shire Restoration Initiative integrates community feedback and course correction throughout implementation:

### 1. Monthly Community Meetings (Open to All Residents)

**Purpose:** Share progress updates; gather community input; identify emerging issues

**Structure:**
- 15 minutes: Project updates from staff
- 15 minutes: Q&A
- 30 minutes: Open discussion and feedback
- Refreshments and informal conversation

**Outcome:** Community concerns elevated to staff meetings for response

**Example:** If community members report certain families aren't accessing mental health services due to stigma, staff will design alternative outreach approaches (home visits, different terminology, community education)

### 2. Project Advisory Committee (Monthly Meetings)

**Members:** 12 community representatives including:
- Displaced family member
- Farmer
- Business owner
- Elder (70+)
- Youth (under 25)
- Woman
- Veteran
- Geographic diversity (different neighborhoods)

**Role:** Review detailed progress data; provide feedback on what's working and what isn't; suggest course corrections

**Authority:** Advisory (not decision-making), but staff commit to seriously considering all recommendations

**Example:** If Advisory Committee notes that tree-planting events aren't attracting young families, staff might adjust timing, add childcare, or incorporate children's activities

### 3. Quarterly Adaptive Management Reviews (Staff + Partners)

**Process:**
1. **Data Review:** Examine all evaluation data from previous quarter
2. **What's Working?** Identify successful strategies to amplify
3. **What's Not Working?** Identify problems, low participation, or unmet targets
4. **Why?** Analyze root causes of challenges
5. **Adjust:** Make concrete changes to activities, approaches, or priorities
6. **Document:** Record decisions and rationale

**Example Scenario:**
- **Data:** Only 30% of displaced families have moved into permanent housing by Month 9 (below target)
- **Analysis:** Construction slower than projected due to winter weather delays
- **Adjustment:** Shift timeline expectations; add second construction crew; prioritize families with children for completed homes

### 4. Participant Feedback Mechanisms (Ongoing)

**Methods:**
- **Suggestion Box:** Anonymous written suggestions at project office and community events
- **One-on-One Check-Ins:** Staff regularly ask participants "How is this working for you? What would make it better?"
- **Post-Event Surveys:** Brief surveys after community events asking what participants liked and what to improve
- **Open-Door Policy:** Any community member can meet with Gandalf or Sam to discuss concerns

**Response:** Staff review feedback weekly; implement quick improvements immediately; discuss larger changes at staff meetings

**Example:** If apprentices report master craftspeople aren't providing enough hands-on practice, coordinators will work with masters to adjust teaching approach

### 5. Cultural Responsiveness

**Recognition:** Evaluation methods must respect Hobbit culture and values

**Practices:**
- **Oral options:** For Hobbits uncomfortable with written surveys, offer oral/interview alternatives
- **Informal settings:** Conduct interviews over tea and biscuits, not sterile office environments
- **Community-friendly language:** Avoid jargon; use plain Hobbit-speech
- **Respect for privacy:** Never pressure Hobbits to share trauma; always allow opt-out
- **Honor Hobbit time:** Don't over-burden with surveys; keep instruments short and simple

### 6. Learning and Knowledge Sharing

**Internal Learning:**
- Quarterly "lessons learned" documentation
- Staff share successful strategies and challenges
- Explicit reflection on what would be done differently

**External Sharing:**
- Final evaluation report shared with Elrond Foundation and other communities
- Case studies of successful strategies for replication elsewhere in Middle-Earth
- Presentations at Council of Elrond gatherings about post-war recovery best practices
